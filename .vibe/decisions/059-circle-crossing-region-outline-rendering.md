# circle-crossing tube rendering: trace the outline of a filled passage region, not per-cell segment assembly

**Context:** Backlog item 051 — after three full redesigns of the `circle-crossing` tube renderer (ADR 055, 056, 057, 058), the user still saw visibly disconnected fragments (floating hairpins/capsules) on real generated mazes, even though every redesign passed automated connected-component analysis and the implementer's own visual inspection. Every one of those designs shared the same construction principle: assemble the tube boundary from thousands of per-cell line/arc pieces and guarantee — by careful formula design — that adjacent pieces land on exactly the same points. That guarantee is trivial on the rectangular grid (every cell is identical, so meeting points coincide by symmetry — `rectangle-crossing` works for that structural reason, though it offers no general guarantee either) but has an unbounded set of failure cases on the growing-sector polar topology, where every ring has its own angular resolution. Each bug fixed just moved the failure to the next unforeseen case. The user explicitly asked to stop patching and rethink how the type should be built.

**Decision:** Rebuild `computeCircleTubeSegments` on the inverse principle: first construct the **passage area as a filled region**, then emit its **outline**.
- In polar (radius, angle) space, every passage primitive is an axis-aligned rectangle: one hub band rectangle per cell at `[midRadius − h, midRadius + h]`, flush with the cell's own true boundary on each *open* tangential side (so tangentially connected same-ring cells merge automatically, sharing a full boundary edge) and set back by a cap inset (`0.5 − h` physical, the same margin as the rectangular tube's own hub, clamped to the nearest door edge) on each *closed* one — so a closed tangential wall renders as two separate rounded pipe caps with a visible gap of white between the neighboring tubes, the same closed-pipe reading as `rectangle-crossing`, instead of one shared line inside a continuous band. Plus one connector rectangle per open radial connection (parent's outer hub edge to child's inner hub edge, spanning the child-sized door angles) and entrance/exit connectors. The cap inset was added after the user's own review of the first region-based rendering ("je ne vois pas de tube"): with full-width bands and shared closed walls — the style inherited from ADR 058 — corridors' white flows through closed walls and nothing reads as a closed pipe; this, rather than any geometric micro-gap, plausibly explains the whole history of "the tubes aren't closed" reports.
- The boundary is extracted from the exact union of those rectangles on the grid of their own radial/angular breakpoints: an edge is emitted exactly where passage meets non-passage. Constant-angle edges map to radial lines, constant-radius edges to arcs — the exact same `TubeSegment` vocabulary the PDF/SVG layers already consume, unchanged.
- The outline of a filled region is closed by construction: a floating fragment, dangling stub, or unclosed tube is geometrically impossible, for every maze size, seed, and configuration — the whole bug class ADR 055–058 kept chasing cannot be expressed in this construction.
- The only thing the region alone cannot express — a crossing's weave marks, cutting visibly across passage area — is added as **forced edges**: explicit extra edges fed into the same collinear-merging pass as the natural outline, whose endpoints land on region-emitted arcs by construction (their radii are the very hub-band radii those arcs are drawn at). For a tangential-over crossing, arcs seal the over tube's two edges across the under corridor's door spans; for a radial-over crossing, two bridge sides run through the hub interior, each jogging at mid-radius from the inward door's angle to the outward door's (the open child may sit off-center, and the two doors' angular widths always differ since each derives from its own ring's physical radius — when they align, the collinear halves merge back into one straight line). An earlier draft used per-rectangle over/under labels with a label-pair emission rule instead; it was dropped because a corridor shared by two adjacent crossings needs to be "under" relative to one and "over" relative to the other, which a single per-rectangle label cannot express — forced edges attach the weave marks to each crossing individually and have no such ambiguity. A second earlier draft also used forced edges for the wall between two tangentially closed neighbors (whose full-width bands abutted exactly); the cap-inset design above made those walls ordinary region boundary instead.
- The entrance and exit door openings suppress the boundary edge that would otherwise cap them shut.
- Corner rounding is applied as a post-pass on the extracted outline: every degree-2 vertex where a radial line meets an arc perpendicularly gets the existing `roundCorner` fillet — skipping vertices whose incident edges are too short to absorb the fillet, vertices inside crossing cells (which need their exact weave shape), and vertices with any other segment tip resting within the fillet's reach (a forced wall landing just beside a door jamb would otherwise be left dangling in the freshly rounded gap). The arc-side shortened endpoint is recomputed on its own circle, keeping shortened arcs exactly concentric.
- One robustness lesson worth recording: collinear merging must group pieces by their shared coordinate within a tolerance *before* sorting by span. Two pieces of the same line computed via different-but-equal float expressions (`(a+b)/2` vs `(k+0.5)*step`) differ by one ULP; sorting on the raw coordinate let them interleave out of span order and the merge silently swallowed one — exactly the kind of invisible coincidence-dependence this redesign exists to eliminate, caught by the closure-invariant test sweep across seeds.

**Reason:** The gap between "every automated check says clean" and "the user still sees broken fragments" was never closed after three attempts; the honest conclusion is that per-piece assembly on this topology has more coincidence cases than can be enumerated and tested. Choosing a construction in which the defect class is unrepresentable replaces an endless verification burden with a structural guarantee. The regression test becomes a checkable invariant (every emitted segment endpoint either meets another endpoint or lands on another segment, across many generated mazes) instead of a hand-built scenario list.

**Rejected alternatives:**
- A fourth iteration of per-cell segment assembly (patching ADR 058): explicitly ruled out by the user ("inutile de tenter de corriger le code").
- Boolean union via an external polygon-clipping dependency: adds a dependency and flattens arcs into polylines; unnecessary because the polar topology makes every primitive an axis-aligned rectangle in (radius, angle) space, so an exact in-house rectilinear union preserves true arcs.
- Rasterization + marching-squares contour extraction: guarantees closure too, but produces approximate, wobbly curves instead of exact arcs/lines, and is far heavier.
- Filled-ink rendering (drawing the walls as filled shapes instead of stroked outlines): changes the visual style of the product for no structural benefit over outline extraction.

**Note:** the same region-outline construction could later replace `rectangle-crossing`'s own per-cell assembly (whose correctness rests on cell uniformity rather than a structural guarantee), but that is out of scope here.
